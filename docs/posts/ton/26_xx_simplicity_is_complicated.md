# Simplicity is Complicated

In case it is not apparent yet, I will admit that I use this blog primarily as a motivation to research a concept that is unclear to me.
And this usually works in the hope to satisfy some weird curiosity.
This time, the concept that is unclear to me is _simplicity_.
<!-- more -->

It fascinates me, because it is really hard to grasp.
One the one hand I admire simple things.
Especially, when an otherwise complicated topic becomes apparent and clear, this is something that fills me with genuine joy.
On the other hand, things that are too simple can feel wrong.
It makes you wonder whether you are not given the whole truth.
This way, doubting the simplicity of your own understanding can destroy your whole worldview.

I remember a conversation with a colleague, explaining them what I appreciate in research papers.
The ideal engineering-science paper for me is based on one simple idea.
This way it is easy to explain on the limited pages, and of course it will only be a good engineering paper if it manages to demonstrate that it is very effective at solving the addressed problem.
But my co-worker answered that I must be careful with this, because reviews can be very challenging.
This left me confused, because it didn't occur to me that something being simple could be seen as low quality.

This illustrates the main confusion:
Why is simplicity a property that can contradict excellence?
But there are more questions:
What is it about simplicity that is so hard to grasp?
And, how can something become so simple, it becomes wrong?

## What is simplicity

In the philosophical literature simplicity is often discussed in the context of theories.
A theory being a theoretical construct that is compared to perceptions of the practical subject at hand.
Here, simplicity is best understood in comparing two theories, with the one that contains less concepts being called _simpler_ [@bakerSimplicity2004].

A commonly cited example is the introduction of Einstein's special relativity.
Before it's introduction, the dominant theory was the Lorentz-Poincaré theory which could explain the same phenomena, but it contained more concepts, such as the ether.
Einstein's theory revolves around the simple idea that the speed of light is constant, and so requires less concepts to explain the same phenomena, and is therefore simpler [@bakerSimplicity2004].

The essence of this is also captured in what is called Occam's razor, which states that
> entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity.[@bakerSimplicity2004]

And the entities in this case are the concepts that are used to explain the phenomena.

These definitions are describing simplicity as a property of theories.
However, it remains to be discussed if and how they also apply to other aspects.
Let's first think about explanations:
I think, here the transfer is relatively straightforward.
An explanation for a perceived observation is something that can almost be described as an ad-hoc theory.
If we for example witness another person making a negative remark about us, we may worry about what the explanation for this is.
Our negativity bias[@baumeisterBadStrongerGood2001] let's us jump to the conclusion that this means the person hates us.
However, the explanation for that may not be very simple, because it would also entail that all the previous times, when they were nice to us, they just pretended to like us.
And a simpler explanation would be that they simply had a bad day or their negative comment only applies to the one thing we did and not to our person in general.
In ways like this I think, the preference for simpler explanations can even help to stop worrying sometimes.
